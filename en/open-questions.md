# Open Questions

## Creativity and Evaluation

Evaluation of AI-generated work cannot rely only on visual novelty. Process, human intention, training data, model constraints, and audience context all need to be part of evaluation.

- Should finished objects, production processes, live performance, and audience experience use the same criteria?
- How can repeatability coexist with chance and failure as artistic values?

## Authorship and Collaboration

Authorship and responsibility may involve human artists, model developers, data contributors, prompt writers, tool builders, and exhibiting institutions. The balance differs by work.

- How should credit and compensation recognize performers who interpret or repair model output?
- When archived bodies and voices train new systems, are original performers co-authors, data contributors, rights holders, or all three?

## Consent and Compensation

Consent, opt-out, compensation, deletion, and auditing for artworks and personal data in training sets remain unresolved.

- Does opt-out stop future collection, remove existing records, or require unlearning from trained models?
- Can aggregate disclosures such as EU training-content summaries let individual creators verify use of their works?

## Style Mimicry and Protection

Technical tools such as Glaze and Nightshade are important, but they are not sufficient without law, platform policy, and community governance. Their effectiveness varies with model, preprocessing, version, and image transformation.

## Provenance, Labeling, and Trust

Provenance standards such as C2PA are promising, but metadata can be stripped, and signatures do not by themselves guarantee truthfulness of the represented scene.

- How should training-data lineage, generation provenance, and editing/distribution history connect without collapsing into one "AI" label?
- Can provenance infrastructure avoid excluding small creators and communities unable to operate signing systems?

## Language, Translation, and Narrative

Generated text alone hides vocabulary preparation, translation, dramaturgy, editing, and performance.

- How should translators and translation models be recorded when comparing non-English generated literature?
- Can long-form narrative be evaluated through performability and reader relations rather than similarity to existing literature?

## Bodies, Liveness, and Safety

In robotics, dance, improvisational theatre, and interactive music, latency, misrecognition, breakdown, and physical risk directly affect the work.

- Who holds emergency-stop authority and responsibility for failure?
- How far can a continually learning live work be restaged as the same work?

## Exhibition, Collection, and Preservation

Stateful simulations, external APIs, and cloud-model works cannot be preserved through video documentation alone.

- Which combination of code, weights, prompts, random seeds, data, dependencies, hardware, and display instructions belongs in the collection record?
- After a commercial API closes or changes, should museums emulate, port, or retrain the work?
- How can audience privacy coexist with preserving works that accumulate visitor data?

## Dataset Lifecycles and Reproducibility

OmniArt shows that papers and citations can remain after dataset distribution stops.

- Who should preserve versions, withdrawal reasons, rights status, and alternatives?
- When redistribution is impossible, are datasheets, hashes, statistics, and evaluation protocols enough for verification?

## Cultural and Regional Bias

Relying on English-language and Euro-American sources narrows the history of AI art. More work is needed on Japan, East Asia, Southeast Asia, South Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Latin America.

- How can regional practice be described through language, Indigeneity, migration, class, institutions, and diaspora rather than flattened into nationality or "Global South"?
- What disappears when fragile websites, social media, and oral performance are judged by the archival standards of large Anglophone museums?

## Environmental Cost

The power, cooling, hardware, and waste costs of large models, exhibition infrastructure, and immersive installations remain underdocumented.

- Can work-level assessment include inference, touring transport, projection, climate control, and preservation servers as well as training?

## Markets, Publicity, and Institutional Evaluation

Auctions, prizes, corporate publicity, and platform rankings shape which works become representative "AI art."

- How can regional, collective, and ephemeral practice be evaluated without relying on price, citation, views, or awards?
- How should audits compare claims that "AI made" a work with actual data preparation, editing, production, performance, and maintenance labor?

## Continuing Research Gaps

Continually learning game agents, architecture and fashion production, accessibility practice by disabled artists, primary materials in Middle Eastern, Latin American, and African languages, regional cultural institutions, litigation, and policy updates remain underrepresented. Better coverage requires local-language sources, creator interviews, and preservation checks, not only a larger item count.
