# Open Questions

## Creativity and Evaluation

Evaluation of AI-generated work cannot rely only on visual novelty. Process, human intention, training data, model constraints, and audience context all need to be part of evaluation.

## Authorship and Collaboration

Authorship and responsibility may involve human artists, model developers, data contributors, prompt writers, tool builders, and exhibiting institutions. The balance differs by work.

## Consent and Compensation

Consent, opt-out, compensation, deletion, and auditing for artworks and personal data in training sets remain unresolved.

## Style Mimicry and Protection

Technical tools such as Glaze are important, but they are not sufficient without law, platform policy, and community governance.

## Provenance, Labeling, and Trust

Provenance standards such as C2PA are promising, but metadata can be stripped, and signatures do not by themselves guarantee truthfulness of the represented scene.

## Cultural and Regional Bias

Relying on English-language and Euro-American sources narrows the history of AI art. More work is needed on Japan, East Asia, Southeast Asia, South Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Latin America.

## Environmental Cost

The power, cooling, hardware, and waste costs of large models, exhibition infrastructure, and immersive installations remain underdocumented.

## Areas for Future Expansion

Further research should add AI film, game production, theater, dance, fashion, architecture, education, accessibility, regional cultural institutions, non-English-speaking artists, and updates on litigation and policy.
