# Language, Literature, and Narrative Generation

<div class="topic-lead" markdown="1">
## Conclusion

Machine-written language did not begin with large language models. Combinatorial systems in the 1950s, commercial text generators in the 1980s, recurrent neural networks in the 2010s, and transformer models in the 2020s changed the rules of generation, the source corpora, and the forms of human editing. The useful question is not simply whether a machine wrote a text, but who selected the corpus, filtered the outputs, and turned them into a book, film, performance, or public installation.
</div>

## Three Points

- **Generation and making a work are separate processes.** Selection, sequencing, translation, performance, editing, and publication produce meaning.
- **Conversational fluency is not narrative continuity.** A plausible sentence does not guarantee character, causality, or long-term memory.
- **A corpus is a literary and political apparatus.** Training on nineteenth-century poetry, film subtitles, road data, or published fiction produces different voices and rights questions.

## Historical Development

Christopher Strachey's Love Letters combined phrase templates and vocabularies on the Manchester Mark 1. Racter later foregrounded the computer as author, although research shows that its public narrative of autonomy must be separated from templates, versions of the software, and human editorial work.

Ross Goodwin's projects connected neural language generation to cameras, sensors, actors, thermal printers, and public architecture. Improbotics similarly makes bodily interpretation part of the system by sending generated lines to actors through earpieces.

THEaiTRE documented GPT-2 play generation as research and connected it to a Czech theatre production. Greater fluency does not remove responsibility for structure, bias, copyright, translation, or casting; it makes records of human editorial decisions more important.

## Key Cases

### Love Letters program

<p class="item-meta">1952 · Software/historical case · Foundational · Confidence: High</p>

Strachey's program generated love letters from vocabularies and sentence patterns. Notes and routines are preserved in the [Bodleian Archives](https://archives.bodleian.ox.ac.uk/repositories/2/archival_objects/28661).

### Racter / The Policeman's Beard Is Half Constructed

<p class="item-meta">1983-1984 · Software/book · Foundational · Confidence: Medium</p>

The book was attributed to Racter, developed by William Chamberlain and Thomas Etter. The commercial program was not identical to the version associated with the book, and the complete generating code is unavailable. See the [Electronic Book Review investigation](https://electronicbookreview.com/publications/constructing-the-other-half-of-the-policemans-beard/).

### word.camera, Sunspring, and 1 the Road

<p class="item-meta">2015-2018 · Projects/film/book · Important · Confidence: High</p>

Ross Goodwin used neural language models as parts of a word camera, an AI-scripted short film, and a road novel generated from vehicle sensors and printed during a journey. [Artist archive](https://rossgoodwin.com/) · [1 the Road publisher](https://www.jbe-books.com/products/1-the-road-by-an-artificial-neural)

### Please Feed the Lions

<p class="item-meta">2018 · Public installation · Important · Confidence: High</p>

Es Devlin and Google Arts & Culture transformed words contributed by visitors into lines generated by a model trained on 25 million words of nineteenth-century poetry. [Project archive](https://artsandculture.google.com/project/please-feed-the-lions)

### Improbotics

<p class="item-meta">2016-present · Improvised theatre/research · Important · Confidence: High</p>

Generated dialogue is sent through an earpiece to a human performer, who must justify it physically and emotionally. [Official project](https://improbotics.org/) · [AIIDE paper](https://doi.org/10.1609/aiide.v14i1.13030)

### The Day a Computer Writes a Novel

<p class="item-meta">2016 · Literary project · Contextual · Confidence: Medium</p>

This Japanese project submitted computer-generated fiction to a literary prize, but humans substantially designed plots, vocabulary, rules, and selection. [Japanese interview](https://shinsho-plus.shueisha.co.jp/interview/%E4%BD%90%E8%97%A4%E7%90%86%E5%8F%B2%E3%82%A4%E3%83%B3%E3%82%BF%E3%83%93%E3%83%A5%E3%83%BC/2134)

### THEaiTRE / AI: When a Robot Writes a Play

<p class="item-meta">2020-2021 · Research project/theatre · Important · Confidence: High</p>

A GPT-2 play-generation project was connected to machine translation and a production at Prague's Švanda Theatre. [Theatre archive](https://www.svandovodivadlo.cz/inscenace/673/ai-kdyz-robot-pise-hru) · [Research paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.14668)

## Comparative Questions

- Who discarded and retained generated outputs?
- Are the corpus period, language, genre, and rights status documented?
- Do errors become hidden defects or material for performance?
- How is authorship distributed through translation, voice, and bodies?
- Is audience input participation, or unpaid data provision?
